ACHILLES

Achilles, known as Ἀχιλλεύς (Achilleús) in Ancient Greek, was one of the most celebrated heroes of Greek mythology and is central to Homer’s Iliad. Renowned as the greatest of the Greek warriors during the Trojan War, he was the son of the sea nymph Thetis and Peleus, the king of Phthia and a notable Argonaut. Achilles grew up in Phthia, where he was educated by the wise centaur Chiron and spent his early years with his close friend and companion, Patroclus.

In the Iliad, Achilles leads the Myrmidons, a fierce and loyal tribe, and is depicted as a figure of immense strength and prowess. His most famous act during the Trojan War was the killing of Hector, the Trojan prince and the champion of Troy, outside the city gates. This battle was a defining moment in the epic, showcasing Achilles’s skill in combat and his deep capacity for both rage and grief. However, the Iliad does not cover Achilles’s death, and other ancient sources tell us that he met his end near the conclusion of the Trojan War when Paris, the prince of Troy, shot him with an arrow, often said to have been guided by the god Apollo to strike Achilles in his only vulnerable spot—his heel.

The myth of Achilles’ vulnerability stems from a story that his mother, Thetis, dipped him in the river Styx as an infant, intending to make him invulnerable. However, she held him by his heel, which did not touch the water and remained susceptible to injury. This led to the later legend that Achilles was impervious everywhere except for his heel, which ultimately became his downfall. This story gave rise to the phrase Achilles' heel, referring to a critical weakness in an otherwise strong individual or system. The tendon that connects the calf muscles to the heel is known as the Achilles tendon, named after him and linked to the same myth.

Achilles’s legacy endures as a symbol of unmatched heroism and tragic vulnerability, resonating in literature, art, and language as the embodiment of both the greatest strength and the greatest weakness.
