HELEN

Helen, known in Greek mythology as Ἑλένη (Helénē), or more famously as Helen of Troy, is often regarded as the most beautiful woman in the world. Her story, embedded in the mythological traditions of Greece and Rome, has inspired countless works of art and literature. Helen was said to be the daughter of Zeus and either Leda or Nemesis, and the sister of notable figures such as Clytemnestra, Castor, Pollux, Philonoe, Phoebe, and Timandra. Her beauty was so profound that it caused a chain of events leading to one of the greatest wars in mythology: the Trojan War.

Helen was married to King Menelaus of Sparta, with whom she had a daughter, Hermione. Some sources also claim she was the mother of Nicostratus. The famous story of Helen’s abduction by Paris of Troy is considered the spark that ignited the Trojan War. While Homer’s Iliad and Odyssey, along with other classical authors like Euripides, Cicero, and Aristophanes, provide rich accounts of her life, they often offer different perspectives on her actions and motivations. In her youth, Helen was briefly abducted by Theseus, an event that was significant in shaping her mythos.

A key moment in her legend is the competition among her suitors for her hand in marriage, which was won by Menelaus. The suitors were bound by the Oath of Tyndareus to defend the chosen husband should Helen ever be taken from him. This oath later played a critical role in uniting the Greek states against Troy when Paris carried Helen off. Whether Helen’s departure was a true abduction or a willing elopement remains a topic of ambiguity, adding complexity to her image as both a tragic figure and a catalyst for war.

In Troy, Helen's reputation varied among sources. Homer portrays her as conflicted, showing moments of regret and attempts to atone for her role in the war, while other accounts depict her as deceitful and even taking pleasure in the chaos she indirectly caused. Some versions suggest that Helen never made it to Troy but instead spent the war in Egypt, evading the conflict altogether. Paris’s death at the hands of the Greeks marked a turning point, and in Homer’s account, Helen was ultimately reunited with Menelaus, who took her back to Sparta. However, in other myths, she ascended to Olympus, joining the gods in a more divine fate. A cult dedicated to Helen developed in places like Laconia and at Therapne, where she was worshipped alongside Menelaus.
