HERCULES

Hercules (known as Heracles in Greek mythology) is one of the most celebrated and legendary heroes of ancient mythology. He is renowned for his immense strength, bravery, and his role in a series of difficult and often impossible feats known as the Twelve Labors of Hercules. These labors were a punishment imposed upon him by King Eurystheus of Tiryns and Mycenae, who sought to break Hercules's spirit and prove his inferiority.

Hercules was the son of Zeus, the king of the gods, and Alcmene, a mortal woman, making him a demigod. His birth was surrounded by divine drama: Hera, Zeus's wife, was furious about her husband's infidelity and sought to make Hercules's life miserable from the start. As an infant, Hercules was attacked by two serpents sent by Hera, but he displayed his remarkable strength by strangling them with his bare hands. This act foreshadowed his future heroics and signaled the divine favor he held despite the goddess's enmity.

The Twelve Labors of Hercules were a series of tasks that he was required to complete as penance for killing his wife, Megara, and their children in a fit of madness, which had been caused by Hera's interference. These labors were considered impossible for any man, but Hercules's strength, cleverness, and unwavering determination allowed him to accomplish them. The tasks included slaying the Nemean Lion and using its indestructible hide as armor, capturing the Ceryneian Hind, cleaning the Augean Stables in a single day, and obtaining the Girdle of Hippolyta, the Queen of the Amazons. Other labors involved dealing with the Stymphalian Birds, the Cretan Bull, and capturing the Erymanthian Boar, among others. The final labor was to capture Cerberus, the three-headed guard dog of the Underworld, and bring him to the surface.
