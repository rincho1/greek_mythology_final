EURYTHEUS

Eurystheus (Ancient Greek: Εὐρυσθεύς, romanized: Eurystheus) is a prominent figure in Greek mythology, most well-known as the king of Tiryns and Mycenae and for being the instigator of the famous Twelve Labors of Heracles (Hercules). Eurystheus's role in the myth of Heracles is central to understanding how the hero's journey was shaped by divine retribution, punishment, and the relentless pursuit of atonement.

Eurystheus was a cousin of Heracles, the son of Sthenelus, who was a descendant of Perseus, and Nicippe. He was born before Heracles, and due to the circumstances of their births, Eurystheus became the rightful king of Mycenae and Tiryns instead of Heracles. This discrepancy occurred because Zeus had promised the throne of Mycenae to Alcmene’s son, Heracles, before he was born. However, Hera intervened by delaying Heracles's birth so that Eurystheus would be born first, making him the legitimate heir and king.

Eurystheus was characterized as a cowardly and insecure ruler, contrasting sharply with Heracles’s bravery and heroism. This difference in their characters contributed to the tension between them, especially when Heracles’s fame grew due to his heroic deeds. Eurystheus's jealousy and fear of Heracles's power led him to impose the Twelve Labors upon the hero as a punishment and means of demonstrating his authority. The Labors were not just a test of Heracles's strength, skill, and resilience; they were also intended to break his spirit and prove his subservience.

The Twelve Labors of Heracles included daunting tasks such as slaying the Nemean Lion, capturing the Ceryneian Hind, cleaning the Augean Stables, and obtaining the Girdle of Hippolyta, among others. Each labor was designed to be impossible or extremely dangerous, pushing Heracles to his limits. Despite the severe challenges, Heracles completed each task, showcasing his heroism, intelligence, and determination. The completion of the labors not only secured Heracles's reputation as one of the greatest heroes of all time but also underscored Eurystheus’s own cowardice and envy.
