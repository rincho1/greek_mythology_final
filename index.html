GREEK MYTHOLOGY

INTRODUCTION

Greek gods and mythology. Stories that instruct people about something significant and meaningful are called myths. They were frequently used to educate people about things like disease and death, earthquakes and floods, and other occurrences that they might not always comprehend. Although they differ slightly, legends and myths are similar. However, Greek mythology is not limited to the tales of gods and goddesses. Human heroes are equally important and include Heracles (also known as Hercules), the explorer who completed twelve impossible tasks for King Eurystheus (and was later worshipped as a god for his achievement); Pandora, the first woman whose curiosity brought evil to mankind; Pygmalion, the king who fell in love with an ivory statue; Arachne, the weaver who was turned into a spider for her conceit; the handsome Trojan prince Ganymede, who became the cupbearer for the gods; Midas, the king with the golden touch; Narcissus, the young man who fell in love with his own reflection; and Kratos, a god of strength and power. Manticores and unicorns, Gorgons, pygmies, minotaurs, satyrs, and dragons of all kinds, the winged horse Pegasus, the horse-man Centaur, the lion-woman Sphinx and the bird-woman Harpies, the one-eyed giant Cyclops, and automatons—metal creatures given life by Hephaestus—are also common monsters and "hybrids" (human-animal forms) in the stories. Many of these animals are now nearly as well-known as the heroes, goddesses, and gods that they are based on. For thousands of years, Greek mythology's characters, tales, themes, and morals have influenced literature and the arts. They can be found in works like Dante's Inferno, Renaissance artworks like Botticelli's Birth of Venus and Raphael's Triumph of Galatea, Romantic poetry and libretti, and the soundtracks of more current plays, films, and books. D'Aulaires' Book of Greek Myths, which was released in 1962 and is still in print today, contains a large portion of the mythology.
